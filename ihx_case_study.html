02 — Problem Framing

The Brief vs The Real Problem

Assumption
This is a UI problem. Make it look better. Clean up the UI.
VS
Root Cause
Information architecture problem. A multi-stage, two-team operation flattened into 4 buckets.
The Brief

Redesign the dashboard. Make it look better.

Clean up the UI. Better visual design. The brief was surface-level and treated a structural problem as a cosmetic one.

The Real Problem

A multi-stage, two-team operation hidden inside 4 buckets.

Pre-auth, enhancement, discharge, settlement, reconciliation across 35 payers — all flattened into one screen that reflected the logic of the company that built it, not the people using it.

The platform had been inherited from a parent insurance company. When IHX became its own entity, nobody had gone back and asked how hospital teams actually work. The screen reflected the logic of the company that built it — not the people using it.

04 — User Understanding

A hospital insurance operation
is not one workflow. It is two.

I spoke with account managers, sat with the PM, and had direct conversations with hospital staff. Two distinct operations — each with different pressures, timelines, and a completely different relationship with the platform.

⚡ Time Critical · Patient Facing

Pre-Auth Team

  • Initiates the claim before the patient is admitted
  • Responds to insurer queries on missing documents
  • Raises enhancement requests mid-treatment
  • Initiates discharge when patient is medically ready
Every unanswered query increases TAT. A delayed discharge costs the hospital a bed per hour.
💰 Finance Critical · Settlement Focused

Claims Team

  • Submits the formal claim after discharge is approved
  • Initiates settlement in bulk at end of day
  • Resolves finance-level queries on settlement timeline
  • Reconciles payments against hospital contracted tariff
Every delay means money already spent on care sitting unreturned, impacting operational capital.
⚡ Management Critical · CXO Facing

Supervisors & Team Heads

  • Opens dashboard to understand team workload at a glance
  • Does not take action directly — oversight only
  • If a query sits unanswered, calls staff immediately
  • Needs multiple sub-statuses visible simultaneously
The dashboard is a supervision tool, not an action tool. They are accountable for team turnaround time.
Two teams. Two urgency profiles. Two completely different reasons to open the platform. The old UI treated them as one user with one need.

06 — Design Decisions

Key decisions and why.

01
Separated PA View and Claim View at primary navigation

Two tabs, two user groups, two bucket hierarchies. Pre-Auth buckets are timestamped — Awaiting Discharge shows today and tomorrow separately. Claims buckets are settlement-state-oriented. Navigation is a single click; cross-view search results redirect automatically.

Core IA Decision
02
Nested bucket hierarchy with consistent visual cues across all modules

The bucket card — primary label, count, urgency indicator, secondary filter chips — communicates stage, volume, and action at a glance. Colour coding is consistent: indigo for approval states, amber for queries, teal for discharge, red for denied. Users learn the system once and it works everywhere. The same component was reused across 7 modules with no additional design or dev work.

Bucket component across modules — PA View · Claim View · Gov Scheme · Corporate · Recon · Mobile · Analytics
Scalable Visual Design
03
Secondary filters evolved from single-select chips to checkbox multi-select

Initial design used horizontal tag chips — clean for single selections. Testing revealed supervisors needed to select multiple statuses simultaneously. Evolved to checkbox-based multi-select in a dropdown — serves both the staff member needing a focused filter and the supervisor needing a cross-status overview.

Testing Driven Change
04
Cross-view search bridge for empty state handling

Splitting views created a problem. A Claim Number search in Claim View returns empty results because the claim lived in Pre-Auth View. Users assumed the claim was missing. Fix: when a search returns no results, the platform shows how many results exist in the other view with a direct link to switch. One line of copy. Now one of the most-used interaction patterns post-launch.

Core Design Decision
05
Follow-up feature — from workflow fix to contract requirement

Hospital staff were leaving IHX to send follow-up emails from their inbox when claims sat in Approval Pending with no insurer response. Introduced email follow-up through a CTA in the actions column with elapsed time. Configurable per payer in Manage Configuration — a fixed default would have been wrong for most hospitals. This feature was a dealbreaker for several enterprise hospitals during contract negotiations.

09 — Constraints, Tradeoffs & Stakeholder Management

What I worked around, held on,
and let go.

Engineering Constraint — Global search was infeasible. I used GA data to figure out what to build instead.

Shipped
Situation

Hospitals had been requesting global search since the platform launched. Engineering assessed ElasticSearch as infeasible — too expensive and architecturally complex at this scale. Non-negotiable constraint.

How I approached it

Pulled Google Analytics event data on column-level searches. Patient Name: 68% · Claim Number: 20% · UTR: 12%. Over 80% of searches fell into three parameters. Built scoped search across those three — data-backed, constraint-aware, faster in practice.

Result

Scoped partial-match search across Patient Name, Claim Number, and UTR Number. Zero additional infra cost. Covers the real use cases. Typing "yash" surfaces all patients with that string.

Stakeholder Management — One enterprise hospital pushed to remove the Discharge bucket. I held the line. They signed anyway.

Client Retained
Situation

During testing, one enterprise hospital pushed hard to remove the Discharge bucket. Their workflow had specific reasons. The account manager was under pressure to accommodate them.

How I approached it

The platform serves thousands of hospitals on a single interface. Accepting one hospital's structural preference would create inconsistency for every other. Four other hospitals confirmed the Discharge bucket structure was right for the majority.

Result

Held the line, communicated the reasoning through the AM, documented the feedback. Platform IA integrity maintained. The hospital signed anyway.

MVP Scope — Settlement in bulk was painful. We designed it. We did not ship it.

Deliberately Deferred
Situation

Hotjar confirmed the Claims team was doing bulk settlement case by case every evening — a known operational pain point with a designed solution ready.

How I approached it

Prioritised shipping a stable, tested IA first. Bulk Workspace was added to v2 roadmap with documented rationale — deferral was a decision, not a failure.

Result

MVP launched with stable IA. Bulk Initiate Settlement on v2 roadmap with data to justify prioritisation in next sprint.

Design Thinking — The Follow-Up Feature went from workflow fix to contract requirement.

Contract Secured
Situation

Hospital staff were leaving IHX to send follow-up emails from their inbox when claims sat in Approval Pending with no insurer response. Every exit broke the workflow.

How I approached it

The feature itself was straightforward. The real design work was the configuration layer — elapsed time configurable per payer, because different insurers operate on different timelines and each hospital has its own MOU.

Result

Staff send follow-up emails directly from IHX. Configurable per payer. This feature was a dealbreaker for several enterprise hospitals during contract negotiations — not a nice-to-have, a requirement to sign.

Use data to answer design questions, not just validate them.

Search decision came from GA. Stakeholder pushback was settled by cohort testing. If I can get data, I get data first.

Platform decisions have to hold for everyone, not just the loudest user.

One hospital's workflow preference cannot drive structural changes to a platform used by thousands.

Deferring is a decision, not a failure. It needs a reason and a record.

Bulk Settlement was ready. It was deliberately deprioritised and added to v2 with documented rationale.

11 — Retrospective

What do I do differently?

01
Dive deeper into the journey before designing.

Direct observation sessions with hospital teams at the start would have surfaced the two-user-group insight faster. The brief was wrong. The Hotjar data told a different story. Getting there earlier saves rework.

02
Bulk Initiate Settlement should have been in the MVP.

Hotjar showed the real operational cost — Claims team doing bulk settlement case by case every evening. I had the data. I should have made the stronger case for including it in scope.

03
Follow-up feature configuration onboarding needs work.

Some hospitals have not set up the elapsed time configuration per payer, which means the feature is not working at full value for them. Onboarding for configuration-heavy features needs its own design attention.
